# 1. Introduction

Modern vehicles produce characteristic acoustic signatures under different operating conditions. A healthy engine at idle sounds distinctly different from one suffering low oil pressure; normal brake engagement produces a different acoustic profile than worn brake pads; and a clean ignition start-up is audibly distinguishable from a dead battery or faulty ignition coil. For decades, experienced mechanics have relied on these auditory cues as a primary diagnostic tool, using their trained ears to identify faults that might otherwise require expensive sensor suites or OBD-II scanners to detect (Ertuğrul & Zan, 2024; Nasim et al., 2023). The question this paper investigates is whether machine learning can replicate and democratize that skill — and, critically, whether it can do so on hardware cheap enough and small enough to be embedded directly in a vehicle.

The automotive industry is undergoing a rapid transformation toward predictive maintenance and connected vehicle architectures. The global predictive maintenance market for vehicles was valued at approximately USD 4.66 billion in 2024, with projections indicating growth to over USD 23 billion by 2034 at a compound annual growth rate exceeding 17% (Global Market Insights, 2025). Engine health monitoring alone accounts for roughly 34% of the predictive maintenance sensor market, reflecting the centrality of powertrain diagnostics in vehicle reliability. This shift is being driven by the proliferation of IoT-enabled sensors, the maturation of AI-powered analytics, and increasing consumer demand for lower maintenance costs and higher vehicle uptime. Sound-based diagnostics represent a particularly compelling modality within this landscape: microphones are inexpensive, non-invasive, and can capture a wide range of fault signatures — from serpentine belt squeals to ignition misfires — without requiring physical contact with engine components (Nasim et al., 2023; Suman et al., 2022).

Recent work in audio-based vehicle fault detection has demonstrated the viability of machine learning for this task. Ertuğrul and Zan (2024) achieved over 92% accuracy classifying engine faults using MFCC features and an Extreme Learning Machine classifier across multiple vehicle models. Nasim et al. (2023) proposed an intelligent sound-based early fault detection system that first performs binary classification (healthy vs. faulty) before identifying specific fault types. Other studies have explored convolutional neural networks operating on mel-spectrograms for real-time vehicle sound detection (Wang et al., 2022). However, the overwhelming majority of this work assumes server-side inference with GPUs or, at most, edge devices with substantially more computational resources than a microcontroller. The deployment of trained audio classifiers on microcontroller units (MCUs) — including the full pipeline of on-device audio capture, feature extraction, and quantized neural network inference — remains underexplored.

This deployment gap matters because many of the practical advantages of audio-based diagnostics are only fully realized at the edge. Cloud-based inference introduces network latency, requires reliable connectivity, and raises privacy concerns since raw audio must be transmitted off-device. By contrast, on-device inference processes audio locally and never transmits it, preserving privacy by design. It operates in environments without network access — a common scenario in repair shops, rural areas, and moving vehicles — and provides near-instantaneous classification results. These advantages align with the broader paradigm of Tiny Machine Learning (TinyML), which brings machine learning capabilities to severely resource-constrained edge devices operating with less than 1 MB of flash memory and limited SRAM (Lin et al., 2023; Abadade et al., 2023). TinyML systems can achieve inference latencies as low as 0–5 ms while consuming milliwatts of power, enabling always-on intelligent sensing in battery-powered or energy-harvesting devices (Tsoukas et al., 2024). Key enabling techniques include model quantization — converting 32-bit floating-point weights to 8-bit integers — along with pruning and knowledge distillation, which can achieve compression ratios of up to 49× while preserving acceptable accuracy (Abadade et al., 2023).

Despite these advances, deploying an audio classifier on a typical MCU presents severe engineering constraints. The target hardware for this study is the Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense Rev2, built around the Nordic nRF52840 system-on-chip featuring an Arm Cortex-M4F processor clocked at 64 MHz. The board provides 1,024 KB of flash memory (983 KB usable) and 256 KB of SRAM — resources that must be shared among the operating system, runtime libraries, audio capture buffers, feature extraction workspace, and the neural network model itself. After accounting for these system-level requirements, the effective budget for the model is approximately 150 KB of flash for weights and graph structure, and 80 KB of SRAM for the TensorFlow Lite Micro tensor arena during inference. The board's on-board MP34DT06JTR PDM MEMS microphone, with 64 dB signal-to-noise ratio and a practical sample rate of 16 kHz, provides the audio input. These constraints are representative of the broader class of Cortex-M4 devices that constitute the majority of deployed microcontrollers worldwide, making findings on this platform broadly applicable.

This paper presents an end-to-end investigation of automotive audio classification under these constraints. We train and evaluate multiple model architectures — including classical machine learning baselines (Random Forest, SVM), a 2-D convolutional neural network operating on log-mel spectrograms, a 1-D CNN operating on MFCC features, and a depthwise separable CNN (DS-CNN) — on the Car Diagnostics Dataset, a publicly available collection of 1,386 labeled WAV recordings spanning 3 operational states (braking, idle, and start-up) and 13 diagnostic conditions (Ragaie, n.d.). We organize these conditions into a hierarchical taxonomy, with our primary deployment target being a 6-class state-aware scheme (Tier 2) that distinguishes normal operation from fault conditions within each operational state. We apply both post-training quantization (PTQ) and quantization-aware training (QAT) to convert float32 models to full-integer int8 representation, deploy the best-performing quantized model on the Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense Rev2 using TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers with CMSIS-NN optimized kernels, and evaluate the complete system across four dimensions: PC-based classification accuracy, quantization impact analysis, on-device performance benchmarking, and end-to-end system evaluation using speaker-based audio playback.

The study is organized around four research questions:

1. **Feasibility.** Can a quantized neural network classify car diagnostic sounds on a Cortex-M4 microcontroller — does the model fit within the memory budget, and does it run in real time?

2. **Quantization impact.** What is the accuracy cost of int8 quantization for small audio classification models, and is there a statistically significant difference between post-training quantization and quantization-aware training?

3. **Domain gap.** How does the speaker-to-microphone domain gap — the acoustic difference between evaluating a model on PC with original recordings versus playing those recordings through a speaker and capturing them with the on-board microphone — affect real-world deployment accuracy?

4. **Data augmentation.** Does training with real-world noise augmentation improve model robustness for deployment compared to synthetic augmentation alone?

These questions are designed to address not only whether on-device automotive audio classification is technically feasible, but also to provide quantitative guidance on the practical trade-offs involved in deploying such a system. The contribution of this work to BDA 602 is an end-to-end pipeline — from raw audio preprocessing through model training, quantization, and microcontroller deployment — accompanied by a rigorous four-dimensional evaluation framework that captures both algorithmic performance and real-world deployment characteristics.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the data sources, including the Car Diagnostics Dataset, the preprocessing pipeline, the data augmentation strategy, and the noise collection process. Section 3 presents the methodology, covering model architectures, quantization procedures, the deployment pipeline, and the evaluation framework. Section 4 provides analysis and interpretation of the experimental observations. Section 5 reports quantitative results in tabular and graphical form. Section 6 discusses implications for industry applications and deployment recommendations. Section 7 concludes with answers to the research questions, limitations, and directions for future work.
