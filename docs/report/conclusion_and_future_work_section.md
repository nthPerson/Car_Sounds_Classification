# 8. Conclusion and Future Work

## 8.1 Summary of Findings

This study investigated the feasibility of deploying a quantized neural network for real-time automotive sound classification on a severely resource-constrained microcontroller. Four research questions guided the investigation, each addressing a distinct dimension of the end-to-end pipeline from training through on-device inference. The findings are summarized below.

### RQ1: Can a quantized neural network classify car diagnostic sounds on a Cortex-M4 microcontroller?

The answer is affirmative. The M6 depthwise separable convolutional neural network (DS-CNN), quantized to int8 via post-training quantization, achieves an F1 macro score of 0.7835 on the six-class Tier 2 classification task while operating entirely within the hardware constraints of the Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense Rev2. The quantized model occupies 21.3 KB of flash—well below the 150 KB model budget—and requires a 63.6 KB tensor arena, fitting within the 80 KB SRAM allocation. Total resource utilization remains modest at 319 KB of flash (33% of available capacity) and 179 KB of SRAM (68%). The complete inference cycle, encompassing 1.5 seconds of PDM audio capture, CMSIS-DSP mel-spectrogram extraction, and int8 TensorFlow Lite Micro inference, completes in approximately 2.5 seconds on the 64 MHz Cortex-M4F processor with no external hardware or cloud dependencies. These results demonstrate that meaningful multi-class audio classification is achievable on a microcontroller costing approximately $30, using only its on-board MEMS microphone.

### RQ2: What is the accuracy cost of int8 quantization for small audio classification models?

The accuracy cost is negligible. Across all 15 quantized model variants—spanning three architectures (M2 2D-CNN, M5 1D-CNN, M6 DS-CNN), three classification tiers, and two quantization methods (PTQ and QAT)—no model-tier-method combination exhibits a statistically significant change in classification accuracy. McNemar's test yields p > 0.05 for every comparison between float32 and int8 predictions. The maximum observed F1 delta is −0.015 (M5 PTQ on Tier 2), and prediction agreement between float32 and int8 models ranges from 95% to 99%. These results extend prior findings on the sufficiency of post-training quantization—previously demonstrated for large-scale architectures such as MobileNet and ResNet (Jacob et al., 2018; Krishnamoorthi, 2018)—to models with fewer than 15,000 parameters. Notably, quantization-aware training (QAT) provides no measurable benefit over PTQ at this model scale. In fact, QAT changes more predictions than PTQ (92–97% agreement versus 97–100%) while failing to improve F1, suggesting that the gradient noise introduced by fake-quantization nodes during QAT fine-tuning is counterproductive for models that are already small enough to quantize losslessly.

### RQ3: How does the speaker-microphone domain gap affect real-world deployment accuracy?

The domain gap introduced by speaker-based playback testing is substantial, though the evidence suggests it is attributable to the speaker hardware rather than the classifier itself. When test audio is played through a Bluetooth speaker (JBL Clip series) and captured by the Arduino's on-board PDM microphone, F1 macro drops from 0.7835 (PC-based int8 evaluation) to 0.5465, a degradation of 30.3%. A speaker ablation study reveals that the playback medium is the primary driver of this gap: laptop speakers, which roll off below 300 Hz, reduce F1 macro to 0.154 (80.3% degradation), whereas the Bluetooth speaker restores low-frequency content and achieves a 2.7× improvement in F1 over the laptop condition. Two fault classes—Braking Fault and Start-Up Fault—achieve recall through Bluetooth playback that matches their PC-evaluated levels, indicating that when the relevant frequency content is preserved, the classifier performs as expected. These findings carry an important practical implication: in a real deployment scenario, where the microphone is positioned near an actual engine rather than a speaker, the acoustic signal path would not include the frequency-distorting intermediary, and classification accuracy should approach the PC-evaluated baseline.

### RQ4: Does real-world noise augmentation improve model robustness?

The results are mixed with respect to clean-test-set performance, though the augmentation pipeline provides a foundation for deployment robustness. The dominant factor in model improvement was class balancing, not noise injection. Condition B (synthetic waveform augmentation with class balancing) produced the largest single improvement, raising M6 DS-CNN's Tier 2 F1 from 0.571 to 0.777—a gain of 0.206. Condition C, which added real-world noise injection at controlled signal-to-noise ratios using 360 noise samples recorded from a 2008 Volkswagen Jetta through the same Arduino hardware, yielded a marginal change on the clean test set (M6 F1: 0.777 → 0.739, Δ = −0.038). Condition D (combined synthetic and noise-augmented data) was optimal only for M2 (F1 = 0.751), not for M5 or M6. The absence of a measurable benefit on clean test data does not preclude a benefit under noisy deployment conditions; however, this study did not directly evaluate models on noisy test sets at controlled SNR levels, so the robustness benefit of real-world noise injection remains an open question for future work.

## 8.2 Limitations

Several limitations constrain the generalizability and precision of the findings reported in this study.

The most significant limitation is the size and provenance of the dataset. The Car Diagnostics Dataset comprises only 1,386 recordings, of which 970 are used for training and 208 for testing. Class imbalance is severe, with a 12.8:1 ratio between the most and least frequent classes (Idle Fault: 552 samples versus Normal Start-Up: 43 samples). Minority classes have very few test samples—Normal Start-Up has 9 and Braking Fault has 11—meaning that a single misclassification can swing per-class recall by 8–11 percentage points. Moreover, the dataset originates from a single Kaggle source, and the recording conditions, vehicles, and microphone hardware used in its creation are not documented. While data augmentation expands the effective training set to over 3,000 samples, all augmented copies for minority classes derive from the same small pool of source recordings, limiting acoustic diversity.

The noise collection effort, while methodologically rigorous in its use of the deployment hardware, is limited to a single vehicle (a 2008 Volkswagen Jetta) recorded in a single session on one day. Different vehicles produce different acoustic signatures, and weather, traffic, and road conditions on the collection day may not be representative of the full range of deployment environments. Rain and extreme weather conditions, which could significantly affect both noise characteristics and vehicle acoustics, were not captured.

On the deployment side, three limitations merit attention. First, the Arduino Mbed OS board package bundles GCC 7.2.1, a 2017-era compiler that lacks support for the SIMD intrinsics required by CMSIS-NN's optimized convolution kernels. This toolchain constraint results in inference times approximately three times slower than what is theoretically achievable with a modern compiler. Second, the playback evaluation methodology introduces a speaker into the signal path that would not exist in a real deployment, making the measured 30.3% F1 degradation a conservative lower bound rather than a representative estimate of real-world accuracy. Third, only the M6 PTQ model was deployed to the Arduino; a multi-model on-device comparison was not performed.

Methodologically, the original unaugmented dataset (Condition A) was not evaluated as a formal ablation condition using the same framework as Conditions B, C, and D, preventing a clean decomposition of the F1 improvement into contributions from dataset expansion versus class balancing. Hyperparameters were tuned on Tier 2 and applied to Tiers 1 and 3 without tier-specific optimization. The study relies on a single train-test split without cross-validation, which introduces variance in the reported metrics—particularly for minority classes. Finally, a TensorFlow version constraint (TF 2.18.0, required due to a cuDNN autotuner issue in TF 2.21 and compatibility requirements with the `tensorflow-model-optimization` toolkit) may affect reproducibility with newer framework versions.

## 8.3 Future Work

Several directions for future research emerge naturally from the limitations and findings of this study.

In the short term, the most impactful improvement would be upgrading the build toolchain. Deploying through PlatformIO or the nRF Connect SDK with GCC 12 or later would enable CMSIS-NN SIMD acceleration, reducing inference latency from 783 ms to an estimated 260 ms—fast enough to support overlapping capture windows and near-real-time update rates. Equally important is on-vehicle field testing, in which the Arduino is positioned near an actual engine rather than a speaker, eliminating the playback domain gap and providing a realistic measurement of deployment accuracy. Additional production-oriented enhancements, such as confidence thresholding and majority voting across consecutive inferences, would improve precision in practice and could be evaluated with minimal additional data collection.

In the medium term, expanding the dataset to include recordings from multiple vehicles—spanning different makes, models, years, and engine types including electric vehicles—would substantially improve generalization. Transfer learning from large-scale audio representations pretrained on datasets such as AudioSet or ESC-50 could boost performance given the limited size of the task-specific training data. Multi-sensor fusion, combining the PDM microphone signal with data from the accelerometer and gyroscope already present on the Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense Rev2, offers a path to more robust fault detection through complementary modalities. A controlled noise-robustness evaluation, in which test clips are mixed with noise at various SNR levels, would directly quantify the benefit of noise augmentation under deployment-like conditions.

Looking further ahead, federated learning across a fleet of instrumented vehicles could enable continuous model improvement without transmitting raw audio, preserving driver privacy. The model's softmax confidence scores could support open-set anomaly detection, flagging unknown fault types as low-confidence predictions across all known classes. A BLE mesh network of Arduino-based sensors reporting to a central dashboard would enable fleet-wide vehicle health monitoring, and integration with existing vehicle diagnostic interfaces such as OBD-II or CAN bus could provide immediate driver notification when faults are detected.

## 8.4 Concluding Remarks

This work demonstrates that a complete audio classification pipeline—from raw PDM microphone input through mel-spectrogram feature extraction to quantized neural network inference—can run entirely on a $30 Cortex-M4 microcontroller with no cloud connectivity, no external accelerators, and no compromise in quantization accuracy. The M6 DS-CNN model, with only 8,166 parameters and a 21.3 KB int8 footprint, achieves an F1 macro of 0.7835 on a six-class automotive fault classification task while consuming just 33% of available flash and 68% of available SRAM. Post-training quantization is sufficient; quantization-aware training provides no additional benefit at this model scale. The primary obstacle to real-world deployment is not the classifier itself but the acoustic domain gap introduced by the evaluation methodology and the constraints of a legacy compiler toolchain—both of which are addressable through straightforward engineering effort. As edge AI hardware matures and automotive datasets grow, microcontroller-based acoustic diagnostics has the potential to become a practical, privacy-preserving, and cost-effective tool for vehicle health monitoring.
