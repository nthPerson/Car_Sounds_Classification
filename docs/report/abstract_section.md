# Abstract

Automotive fault diagnosis traditionally relies on expensive OBD-II scanners or the trained ear of an experienced mechanic, yet many common faults — worn brake pads, low oil pressure, ignition failures — produce characteristic acoustic signatures that a machine learning classifier could detect using only a microphone and a low-cost microcontroller. This paper investigates whether a quantized neural network can perform real-time car sound classification on the Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense Rev2, an edge device constrained to a 64 MHz Cortex-M4F processor, 1 MB of flash, and 256 KB of SRAM. We train and evaluate five model architectures — Random Forest, SVM, a 2D-CNN, a 1D-CNN, and a depthwise separable CNN (DS-CNN), plus their int8 quantized variants — on the Car Diagnostics Dataset (1,386 WAV files spanning 13 conditions across three operational states: braking, idle, and start-up), organized into a three-tier classification taxonomy culminating in a six-class state-aware target task. Among all candidates, the DS-CNN with post-training quantization (PTQ) achieves the best balance of accuracy and efficiency, reaching a Tier 2 macro F1 of 0.7835 with only 8,166 parameters and a 21.3 KB model footprint. Quantization is nearly lossless: no model–tier–method combination produces a statistically significant accuracy change (McNemar's test, all *p* > 0.05), and quantization-aware training (QAT) confers no benefit beyond PTQ for models at this scale. Deployed end-to-end on the Arduino — including PDM audio capture, on-device mel-spectrogram extraction via CMSIS-DSP, and int8 TFLite Micro inference — the system occupies 319 KB of flash (33%) and 175 KB of SRAM (68%), completing a full classification cycle in 2,470 ms (785 ms inference, 197 ms feature extraction, 1,490 ms audio capture). A speaker-to-microphone playback evaluation reveals a 30.3% F1 degradation through a Bluetooth speaker (F1 0.5465, accuracy 68.3%) and an 80.3% degradation through laptop speakers, whose frequency response rolls off below 300 Hz; this speaker ablation study confirms that playback hardware quality, not the classifier, is the dominant source of the domain gap. Additional contributions include a real-world noise corpus of 360 samples collected from a 2008 Volkswagen Jetta using the deployment hardware, a data augmentation study showing that synthetic waveform augmentation with class balancing improves DS-CNN F1 by +0.206, and documentation of a CMSIS-NN SIMD toolchain limitation in the Arduino GCC 7.2.1 environment not previously reported in the literature.
